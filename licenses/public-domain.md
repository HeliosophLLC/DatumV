# Public Domain

Works flagged as **public domain** carry no copyright restrictions. They have
either been formally dedicated to the public domain by their creator (typically
via a Creative Commons CC0 / CC-PDDC dedication, or a plain "this work is in
the public domain" notice) or had their original copyright expire under the
applicable jurisdiction's rules (in the United States, this generally means
works first published before 1929 as of 2026).

## What you can do

- Use the work commercially or non-commercially.
- Modify, redistribute, sublicense, or sell derivative works.
- Use without attribution.

## What you should still do

- **Cite the source anyway.** Attribution is not required, but is essential
  for reproducibility — both your own and that of any downstream user. If your
  model card or paper references a public-domain dataset, name it.
- **Verify the jurisdiction.** Public-domain status under U.S. law does not
  automatically extend to other jurisdictions. For commercial deployment in
  EU / UK / etc., confirm the work's status under the relevant local rules.
- **Distinguish dedication from expiration.** A CC0-dedicated work is
  unambiguous. A "this is in the public domain" assertion by a third party
  (e.g. an aggregator over crowdsourced inputs) is only as reliable as the
  upstream rights chain — the dedication is binding for the dedicator's own
  contribution but cannot retroactively cure rights held by others.

## SPDX identifier

`CC-PDDC` (Creative Commons Public Domain Dedication and Certification) is the
closest SPDX identifier for general "public domain" assertions. Formal CC0
dedications are tagged separately as `CC0-1.0`.
